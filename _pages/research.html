A cell can sense and respond to a variety of cues from within and outside the cell, and I am interested in understanding how these signals are integrated to drive cellular decisions, especially in development and disease. By combining cellular biomechanics with comparative transcriptomics across species, we can uncover how mechanical and genetic programs produce robust yet adaptable cell identities, and devise strategies to precisely reprogram and restore cell identity in disease.

Cellular biomechanics & Mechanobiology

How do single cells respond to mechanical, chemical, and geometric cues in their microenvironments? With collaborators at the University of Cambridge, I have developed a homeostatic mechanics framework that generates cell shape, actin cytoskeletal ordering, and traction maps across different substrates, giving multiple readouts of how cells respond to their surroundings. Using this, I showed that cell alignment on micropatterns is driven by fluctuations in cell shape rather than edge contact. I also applied the framework to predict human mesenchymal stem cell differentiation across varied environments, demonstrating that cytoskeletal free-energy predicts stem cell lineage commitment better than morphological measures alone.

Large-scale analysis of gene expression data

I study how gene expression differs across species at cellular resolution, with a focus on human brain evolution. Using single-cell transcriptomic atlases from five primates, I built a cross-species taxonomy of 57 homologous cell types to identify where human brain cells diverge from our closest relatives. By combining single-cell data with population-scale co-expression networks spanning 19 animals, I pinpointed a set of human-specific genes whose changed activity likely underlies the cognitive distinctiveness of the human brain. I apply this framework across systems, from brain organoids to heart evolution and plants, to reveal how changes in gene expression shape function.
